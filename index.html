Building safer communities

We are developing evidence on how to reduce crime and create safer communities in partnership with governments and police agencies.

Mitigating harms from violent extremist groups

We are learning how to prepare communities and former members of violent extremist groups to return home to live peaceful lives and avoid a return to violence.

Our approach

Deep partnerships

We work with our partners from before programming begins, to understand their challenges and ensure the evidence we develop together informs their decisions.

Credible evidence

We use randomized experiments to quantify the impact of programs and policies — and how they can be more effective. 

Ethical research

We are leaders in developing tools to protect research participants' sensitive answers to surveys about violence and how answering affects mental health.

What we've learned

Recent evidence developed by the lab

Trusted authorities can change minds and shift norms during conflict

We worked with Mercy Corps Nigeria to learn about how to encourage communities to accept returning members of the violent extremist group Boko Haram. In partnership with two leading religious figures, we developed, recorded, and tested radio messages, which shifted willingness to accept former associates of Boko Haram by 10 percentage points.

Community policing does not build citizen trust in police or reduce crime 

We led a six-country evaluation of community policing, to find out whether the widely-adopted policy affects citizen cooperation with police and reduces crime. Working directly with police agencies, we developed locally-appropriate increases in community policing practices. In all six sites, we found that the policy did not live up to its promise and did not reduce crime or build trust.

Impact evaluation tools

We develop tools and methods for more ethical, cost-effective, and credible impact evaluations.

Asking sensitive questions

We developed tools for designing and analyzing list experiments, randomized response questions, and endorsement experiments to protect respondents' privacy when asking sensitive questions.

Research planning tools

Our lab co-led the development of the DeclareDesign software tools and the Model-Inquiry-Data Strategy-Answer Strategy framework for establishing whether a research plan can deliver credible answers to research questions.

Asking about violence

In partnership with researchers in four countries, we are studying the effects of asking about violence on mental health and how to better protect respondents and data collectors asking the questions from harm.

Research team

Graeme Blair
Faculty Director

Blair is Associate Professor of Political Science at UCLA and Co-Director of Trainings and Methods at the researcher-practitioner organization Evidence in Governance and Politics. He has been conducting research on preventing violence in Nigeria since 2009.

  

Elayne Stecher
Research Fellow

Stecher is a joint Ph.D./M.A. candidate in political science and statistics at UCLA. She holds an M.A. in international relations from the University of Chicago. She has lived in Jordan and Nicaragua and has conducted research in Israel/Palestine, Jordan, Nigeria, and Iraq.

   

Mohammed Bukar
Field Research Director

Bukar leads Mobukar Research Consultancy, which is the premier survey research organization in Borno State, Nigeria and conducted the earliest social science surveys since the recapture of Maiduguri from Boko Haram.

    

Our partners

Mercy Corps Nigeria

We worked with the international humanitarian organization Mercy Corps on an evaluation of radio programs to change social norms and behaviors about accepting returning former members of Boko Haram.

United Nations Managing Exits from Conflict (MEAC) project

We have been working with the MEAC project to collect the first systematic survey data on demobilized former members of Boko Haram transiting through UN camps.

Evidence in Governance and Politics (EGAP)

Our faculty director leads EGAP's efforts to build capacity of researchers and measurement and evaluation practitioners in the Global South, especially in Africa and Latin America and to build accessible impact evaluation tools.
